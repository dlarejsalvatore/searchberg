An Overview of Medical Malpractice in New York

July 3, 2017

The medical malpractice law varies slightly from state to state. If you think you or a loved one has been a victim of medical malpractice in New York, you should understand its laws thoroughly.

As far as medical malpractice is concerned, New York has many patient-friendly laws. For instance, unlike some of the other states that often place a cap ($250,000) on the compensation that can be received by a victim, New York does not.

Let’s take a look at other important aspects of medical malpractice in New York:

Statute of Limitations

Every state has a law regarding statute of limitations. Basically, this is the limit within which the affected person can go to the court and file a case. As far as New York is concerned, this limit is 30 months (2.5 years), after which the person loses his right to sue. However, there might be exceptions to this.

The Discovery Rule

One of these exceptions is the discovery rule. This is the scenario when the person could not have possibly known that he/she was a victim of medical malpractice.

As far as New York is concerned, there are quite a few limitations to the discovery rule. It only applies when a foreign object had been left inside the patient’s body.

If this has happened, the medical malpractice lawsuit has to be filed within one year of the discovery.

Statute of Limitations for Minors

For minor children, there is no statute of limitation until they have reached the age of 18.

However, regardless of the age of the child when medical malpractice occurred, it cannot exceed ten years after the incident occurred or the foreign object was discovered in the patient’s body.

Limit on Damages

As mentioned above, the New York State does not place a cap on the amount that the victim can receive.

Shared Fault Fees

According to the New York State’s laws, if a person files a case for medical malpractice and is found to be partially responsible for injuries, the amount can be reduced.

This is according to the ‘pure comparative negligence’ rule. This means that your amount will be reduced in proportion to your fault in the incident.

Certificate of Merit

After the plaintiff has filed for a medical malpractice lawsuit in New York, a certificate of merit has to be written and submitted by the attorney within 90 days of that.

This document must have one of the two verification:

  1. The attorney has reviewed the medical malpractice case and has consulted with at least one physician, and has reached the conclusion that there is reasonable basis for filing the case.
  2. The lawyer tried to consult three separate physicians but none of them agreed to give an appointment.
