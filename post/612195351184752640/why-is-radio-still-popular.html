Why Is Radio Still Popular?

Our taste in music and listening habits may have changed over time, but one thing has remained the same: our love for the old-school radio.

According to a 2019 report by Nielsen, podcasts haven’t been successful in replacing radio, after all. Every week, 92 percent of the US population still listens to the radio—which is nearly 272 million people! These numbers are way above the percentage of users of television and podcasts, which us 87% and 22%, respectively.  

But why is radio still so famous in the age of Spotify and Soundcloud? In this blog, we’ve covered 4 reasons why radio will stay alive for another century, in light of Nielsen’s research.

Easily Accessible and Portable

Radio is popular for being the first medium of audio entertainment for a large audience.

According to Nielsen’s research, up to 69 percent of the total weekly radio is obtained from out-of-home. The traffic increases between 6 AM to 5 PM on a typical workday. During the weekends, the highest consumption is recorded. As for the weekends, the noontime is the busiest, when most people are out with friends or families.

Radio accompanies you whether you’re on your way back home from a hectic day at work, or on your interstate trip with your buddies. And not just that, it’s also there to cheer you up when you’re exhausted doing chores!

A Great Medium for Marketing

The wide number of users has made radio a great channel for brands to communicate their advertisements to customers. People who are too busy to watch television can still be reached via the radio. Advancement in technology has also enabled retail marketers to broadcast advertisements to only those who are in the proximity of their physical store.

It Can Adapt to the Latest Technologies

Radio is extremely flexible when it comes to adopting newer technologies and changing consumer behaviors. Radio is no longer limited to the antennas and receivers; it can now be enjoyed online on laptops, tablets, smartphones, and smart speakers with no restrictions of time and location. So, no twisting and turning the antennas to catch the right radio frequency!  

Caters to a Large Diverse Audience

According to Nielsen’s report, the demand for radio knows no demographic boundaries. It’s heard by people of all ages, belonging to different genders, races, cultures, and ethnicities. Let’s have a look at the percentage of radio users in different consumer segments of the United States:

· Hispanic Americans: 99 percent

· African Americans: 98 percent,

· Women (25–54): 98 percent  

· Teenagers (12–17): 93 percent

Would You Care If Music Disappeared From Radio?

As per a 2018 news article, the music industry has seen a massive surge in recent years and 75% of the revenues come from music streaming. According to an Edison Research report released in 2019, the major contenders fueling the surge are Pandora, Spotify, and Apple Music, with 66 percent of respondents choosing them as their most-used music streaming platforms. Only 8 percent said that they would use a radio station to listen to music.

These statistics may be a sign that these music-based platforms may replace the radio, but would you care if that happened?

Let’s dive in!

Do Radio Lovers Need to Worry?

Here are some reasons why radio lovers won’t care even if music disappeared from the radio:

Radio Was Never About Only Music

Radio can never die; even if music disappears from it.

This can be clearly understood in the words of Larry Rosin, the president of Edison Research,

“If someone only ever liked radio for the music and nothing else, they have moved on. But for the overwhelming majority of radio users then and now … it’s more of a full-flower experience, even including the ads.”

Radio Habits Are Different Than Individual Music Collections

We’d like to take this opportunity to clear one huge misconception: radio listening habits are not similar to that of a person’s music list. And this isn’t our opinion—the numbers prove it. Despite being attacked by countless music-streaming apps, online downloads, and old school CDs and cassettes for many years, radio is still listened to by 90 percent of the total US adult population for an average of 102 minutes—which is way more than the usage rate of smartphones and TV!

Moreover, you can take radio anywhere with you—as the research suggests, 45 percent of the radio listening is done while driving and 21 percent at workplaces!

Radio stations don’t view themselves as a part of the music industry anymore. They don’t just music to the listeners, but they offer companionship throughout their daily commute and routine chores. So, if anyone believes that radio is supposed to play music all the time, they haven’t entirely understood the essence of radio.

Radio Offers a Sense of Community

According to a study by the Bridge Ratings, up to 75 percent of respondents agreed that they feel closely bonded with the radio and that’s one of the major reasons why they cannot let go of it. The factor that remained in the first place was “easy usage,” with approximately 89% people agreeing that’s the reason why they listen to it.

For those who have developed strong radio listening habits, radio is more like a compulsion in their daily routines. You start your car, tune on the radio, and t just keeps going on. It’s not just music; it’s a strong bond right there!

So, Would You Care If Music Vanished From Radio?

Our bet is, if you’re a fan of the entire experience that contemporary radio offers, you wouldn’t care even if music disappeared from it. It’ll still possess the essence of classic detective series, pure comedy, and golden age detective shows that Spotify and other music-based platforms can never provide—no matter how intensely they try to mimic radio.

If you too have felt the strong bond with old-style radio; their classic shows, you know it’s not easy to get over them—and you don’t even have to! The Lesser Known World Of Old-Time Radio

Radio shows broadcast today have a whole new outlook compared to those back in the day. During the Golden Age of Radio, i.e. the 1920s onwards, commercial radio broadcasting took off immediately. It was the beginning of the entertainment industry at the time and was the primary medium of entertainment in the United States.

From listening to the latest in music on a hot summer’s day, to lighting a cigar before listening to the news broadcast of the day, tuning in to listen to the radio was a way of life.

There was a lot more to the radio at the time too; it wasn’t just news and tunes. Old-time radio shows attracted a huge audience back in the day and are beginning to gain popularity once again.

What were old-time radio series?

Since radio was the only source of entertainment for a long time, radio series were launched for all to listen to. These series weren’t like the radio shows we listen to today, with celebrity interviews and music. These shows were well-planned and thought out. They were mainly pre-recorded and were a form of storytelling.

There were many genres launched so that the audience had plenty of choice. These programs incorporated sound effects to give listeners a gripping experience.

Old-time radio series genres

Comedy

Comedy shows have been as popular throughout history. With so much gloom around, families would come together to share a laugh with comedy radio series. Comedy series on the radio were generally hosted by women like the famous “My Favorite Husband” where Lucille Ball and Richard Dennings share eccentric chemistry. Another show based on a witty professional, Miss Brooks covers the interesting and hilarious encounters of this one-of-a-kind teacher. Other famous comedy shows include “Fibber McGee & Molly” and “Abbot and Costello.”

True crime

True crime is a classic genre that has been at the heart of the entertainment industry for decades. Harrowing real-life events are used as inspiration for books, movies, television series, and even radio shows. Shows like “Crime Classics” and “Escape” cover true crime stories straight from the records. Such shows would talk about crime happening all over the world by incredible hosts like Mr. Thomas Hyland. They were the perfect way to inform listeners about what was going on around the world without the monotonous tone of a news report.

Detective

Everyone loves a good thriller, right? Detective radio shows were a league of their own. They are the most popular of all old-time radio series and such shows will always have the audience coming back for more. During the early and mid-1900s, private detective businesses were on the rise. The services of such detectives were employed for a number of reasons like helping with cases in court, solving crime, suspicion, etc. Popular detective series of this time include “The Adventures of Sam Spade” and of course, “The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes.”
